💗 Pelvic Floor & Sexual Health

The most important topic almost nobody talks about. You are not alone, you are not broken, and there is a lot you can do. This page is your starting point.

💡 Why Pelvic Health Changes at Menopause

Your pelvic floor, vagina, and clitoral tissue are densely packed with estrogen receptors. When estrogen declines:

  • Vaginal tissue thins, dries, and becomes less elastic — this is called vaginal atrophy (GSM: Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause)
  • Pelvic floor muscles weaken — contributing to incontinence, a feeling of heaviness, and reduced sensation during intimacy
  • Blood flow to the pelvic region decreases — affecting arousal, lubrication, and the ability to orgasm
  • Natural lubrication drops significantly — making intercourse painful for up to 50% of post-menopausal women

🌸 The good news:

Every single one of these changes is manageable. Pelvic floor exercises, vaginal moisturizers, lubricants, and low-dose vaginal estrogen (prescribed by your doctor) can restore comfort and pleasure at any age.

🦋 How to do a Kegel correctly (most women do it wrong):

  1. Find the right muscle: Imagine you're trying to stop the flow of urine mid-stream or stop passing gas. The muscles that tighten are your pelvic floor. Do NOT do this while actually urinating — it can cause bladder infections.
  2. The squeeze: Tighten those muscles, hold for 3–5 seconds, then fully relax for 5 seconds. One squeeze = one Kegel.
  3. What NOT to do: Don't hold your breath. Don't tighten your butt, thighs, or belly. Keep everything else relaxed. If you're doing it right, no one should be able to tell you're doing it.
  4. Routine: 10 reps, 3 times per day. Progress to 10-second holds over several weeks.

💕 Intimacy After Menopause: A Practical Guide

Sex after menopause can be different — and different doesn't mean worse. Here's what helps:

  • Use lubricant. Lots of it. Water-based for daily use, silicone-based for longer sessions. This is not optional — it's essential care.
  • Vaginal moisturizers (like Replens or hyaluronic acid-based gels) used 2–3 times per week can restore tissue health, not just during sex.
  • More foreplay. It takes longer for blood flow to reach pelvic tissues now. 20+ minutes of arousal before penetration is normal and healthy.
  • Talk to your partner. "My body has changed. I need more time, more lubrication, and a different approach. Here's what feels good now."
  • Consider vaginal estrogen — a low-dose cream, tablet, or ring prescribed by your doctor. It's not systemic HRT. It stays local and restores tissue health with minimal absorption.
  • Pelvic floor physiotherapy — a specialist who can assess your specific pelvic floor tension/pain and give you targeted exercises. Life-changing for many women.

🗣️ Questions for Your Partner

Having an honest conversation is the single most powerful thing you can do. Here are conversation starters:

  • My body is going through changes. I need you to understand that it's not about you — it's hormonal.
  • I need more foreplay now than I used to. Can we take penetration off the table for a while and explore other ways to be intimate?
  • Lubricant is not optional anymore. Can we pick some out together?
  • Sometimes I feel self-conscious about my changing body. I need you to tell me I'm still desirable.
  • If penetration hurts, I will tell you. Please don't take that as rejection — it's my body telling me something.
  • Can we schedule intimacy? I know it sounds unromantic, but when I'm tired and stressed, knowing we have planned time helps me mentally prepare.
  • I'm going to start pelvic floor exercises. Would you be willing to learn about them with me?

❤️ A note for partners:

If your partner is sharing this with you, she is trusting you with something deeply vulnerable. The best response is: "Thank you for telling me. I love you. What do you need from me?"
